\chapter{Experiments}
\section{Data}

In collaboration with the Reed College Registrar, we obtained three years of
anonymized student and class schedule data for the purpose of evaluating our
algorithms. The student data compiles the fall and spring class schedules chosen
by every student registered as a freshman for that year, de-identified by
representing each student with a randomly generated ID number. We use this
information to ensure students are not matched to a section with a time
conflict. The data also includes the students’ top prospective major from their
application to Reed, which we use for sorting students into groups. While the
prospective major may not accurately reflect each student’s chosen major by the
time they are in class, it provides a good baseline for what discipline a
student is interested in, which is sufficient for our group fairness purposes.
The Registrar also provided us with the master class schedules for each year
corresponding to the student data, so we can match students to the actual
sections that were available for their year.

\section{Implementation}

To perform experiments, we implemented each algorithm in Python. We chose Python
for the fact that it is a popular higher-level programming language, which means
it will be easier for others to read and understand should this algorithm
actually be implemented after this thesis is complete. It is also an
object-oriented language, which means we could represent the components of our
model as Python classes with properties, such as a \texttt{Student} class, which holds each
student’s provided data,  and a \texttt{Class} class, which can hold information such as
capacity, enrolled students, and meeting times. The object-oriented approach
helps the implementation more intuitively represent our actual scenario.
Additionally, Python has a very accessible csv library that allows us to parse
our data into these objects. While Python may not be as fast as other languages
with some similar features, we decided that because the \textsc{wt} algorithm performs in
polynomial time (see Theorem \ref{thm:polywt}) and Reed has relatively small class sizes,
the impact on the performance of the algorithms was less essential than the
readability of the code.

\subsection{Synthesizing Preferences}

While we did have access to the student schedule and major data, we were still
missing a key component of a matching problem: rankings. Because there was no
feasible way of acquiring each student’s actual preferences from class
registration years ago, we needed to synthesize student rankings over the HUM
sections to effectively simulate our HUM-assignment environment.

According to conversations with The Registrar throughout this process, Reed
College students are generally less inclined to wake up early and take longer
classes—which means that 50-minute classes held later than 10am tend to be the
most popular choices. To simulate this preference distribution, we used an
implementation of the Mallows Ranking Model \cite{mallows} from the Python library
\textit{PrefSampling} \cite{prefsampling}. This model is a popular way of generating ranking data for
simulating elections, and is one of the more effective methods of doing so
\cite{boehmer}. The Mallows model takes a “central vote” representing the most
popular real-life choices and deviates the outputted rankings from this
according to some dispersion coefficient $\phi = [0, 1]$. A $\phi$ closer to 1 means
rankings that differ significantly from the central vote are more likely to be
generated. We found an effective $\phi$ value for our purposes was 0.4, which allows for
some deviation but overall keeps the rankings more central. In practice, this
manifests in the form of most students having a 50-min MWF section as their top
choice, though there are approximately 35\% of students who have a longer TTh
class as their top choice.

In $\texttt{synthesize\_preferences}$, we iterate through each student’s schedule and find
all of the HUM sections that do not conflict with their other chosen classes
(including the HUM they actually chose that year). Then, we turn those available
sections into a central vote by weighting them by their time and class period
length, where later and shorter sections are weighted higher. We run that vote through
Mallows to generate the student’s preferences over every section that doesn't
have a time conflict. Then, depending on the trial we truncate the list to
a maximum length of 5, 10, or full length to simulate other unknown time
conflicts, as well as the fact that students may not have preference over every
individual section. These are the preferences used when running each algorithm.
A relevant consideration to note here is that because our student schedule data
is from years where students had to conciously make room on their schedule for
their desired HUM sections, it is possible there is a bias over which sections do not have a
time conflict.

\subsection{Notes on the Algorithms}

\textsc{fcfs} and \textsc{greedy} both involve an ordering of students; in
real-life, this is in the form of whichever students are fastest to sign up for
HUM. In our implementation, we randomly shuffle the list of students and then
iterate through to represent this ordering. Note that this means we do not test
the algorithms against an adversarial ordering for \textsc{fcfs} or
\textsc{greedy}, and that both of those algorithms could actually benefit from the
random ordering.

For the \textsc{wt} algorithm, we represent the graph of students to seats as an
\textit{adjacency matrix}. The rows of the matrix represent students, and the columns represent seats
in each section, so the adjacency matrix is of size $|R| \times |S|$. At each
index we calculate the weight of the edge between the student and that seat in
accordance with Definition \ref{weighted-def}. For instance, in
Figure \ref{fig:adj_matrix}, student 0 has weight 0 for seat 0, so we place a 0
at 0,0 in the matrix. A 0 means there is no edge between that student and seat.
Similarly, student 0 has weight 2 for seat 1, so we have a 2 at 0,1.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{bmatrix}
      0 & 2 & 0 \\
      1 & 1 & 1 \\
      0 & 0 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}
  \end{equation*}
  \caption{Adjacency matrix representing 3 students and 3 seats.}
  \label{fig:adj_matrix}
\end{figure}

The adjacency matrix we generate is the input to \textsc{max-weight}. In Section
3.3, we cite the Hungarian algorithm \cite{hungarian} as a specific
\textsc{max-weight} algorithm for computing the runtime in Theorem
\ref{thm:polywt}. In practice, we actually use an algorithm called
\textit{LAPJVsp}, implemented in the SciPy library \cite{scipy}. LAPJVsp also
performs in $O(n^3)$ time theoretically, but in practice is usually much faster
\cite{Jonker}, which is helpful when repeatedly running trials of the algorithm
in our experiments.

The matching returned for all algorithms is a Python dictionary with student IDs as the keys, and the ID of the class they were assigned to as values.

\section{Results}

Because of the randomness in our preference synthesis, being that Mallows
does not necessarily output the same preferences each time, as well as the
randomness involved in the ordering of \textsc{fcfs} and \textsc{greedy}, we ran
many trials to accurately evaluate the algorithms’ performances. The length of
the preference lists was another variable that could affect whether students got
a class they preferred, so we vary the trials by preference list length as well.

We ran 50 trials each on preference lists of lengths at most 5, 10, and full
(not truncated) for all three years of data per algorithm. We want to analyze
the results against the following metrics in each trial, chosen based on our
ISF1M construction (Definition \ref{def:ispim}) and our original motivation for the
project:
\begin{itemize}
  \item
        How well distributed the groups of students are among classes
  \item
        The number of blocking pairs in the final matching
  \item
        The number of unmatched students
  \item
        The average rank a student gives their assignment to indicate student
        satisfaction
  \item
        How balanced sections are in terms of class size
\end{itemize}

Firstly, we can measure how well distributed the groups of students are by
looking at the minimums and maximums for each trial. That is, what is the
minimum number of students of each group in each section, and what is the
maximum? These metrics will indicate to us how group fair the matchings output
by each algorithm are.

Figures \ref{fig:min_students} and \ref{fig:max_students} illustrate how
students are distributed within groups. Notably, in each sampled year, the set
of all students was approximately composed of $20\%$ group A, $30\%$ group B, and
$50\%$ group C. This distribution of
students is reflected in the minimums and maximums. For instance, in Figure
\ref{fig:max_students}, \textsc{fcfs} did not proportionally assign students of
group C as successfully as \textsc{wt} in
comparison to the other groups over all of the years and preference lengths, as
there is a noticeably higher maximum number of group C students shown in each
plot. It is also important that in Figure \ref{fig:min_students}, \textsc{fcfs} and
\textsc{greedy} frequently distributed
students such that almost every trial had sections with 0 students of some
group, whereas in all of the \textsc{wt} trials with preference lists longer
than 5 there was some representation of each group in every class.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.65\linewidth]{figures/groups_violin.png}
  \caption{Comparison between groups of number of students assigned to each
    class for Year 3. (See plots with similar results for other years of sample
    data in the appendix.)}
  \label{fig:group_size}
\end{figure}

We also observe the overall visualization of the distribution of groups
amidst classes for one year of sample data in Figure \ref{fig:group_size}, which shows that
\textsc{wt} creates a much less variable distribution of students. All of
the classes, especially in trials with longer preference lists, have a smaller
difference between the minimum and maximum number of students of each group than
\textsc{fcfs}. \textsc{greedy} has a similarly small distribution, though it
does not manage to assign that many students to each class, with the maximum of
each group being 4 students. Thus, \textsc{wt} accomplishes group fairness much
more successfully than the other two algorithms.

\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.65\linewidth]{figures/blocking.png}
  \caption{Comparison of inter-group blocking pairs present in each trial.}
  \label{fig:blocking_pairs}
\end{figure}

We also are interested in our original conception of stability, which means we want to check how many
inter-group blocking pairs are present in the final matching output by each
algorithm. Figure \ref{fig:blocking_pairs} illustrates that there are 0 blocking
pairs in any matching output by \textsc{fcfs}, which is to be expected by the
results of Theorem \ref{thm:fcfs-stable}. We can also observe that
\textsc{greedy} and \textsc{wt} both tend to have blocking pairs, with
\textsc{wt} consistently having the most, which is also
consistent with Theorem \ref{thm:greedyunstable} and our problem framework
for \textsc{wt} in Definition \ref{def:ispim} that disregards the presence of
inter-group instability.

\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.65\linewidth]{figures/unmatched.png}
  \caption{Comparison of number of unmatched students in each trial.}
  \label{fig:unmatched}
\end{figure}

Relatedly, we check how many students remain unmatched in the final matching
in each trial. While \textsc{wt} may produce the most blocking pairs as seen in
Figure \ref{fig:blocking_pairs}, Figure \ref{fig:unmatched} shows that it does
successfully match every student to a year-long HUM section in all trials. We
can also observe that \textsc{greedy} often leaves between 50-100 students
unmatched, and \textsc{fcfs} always has unmatched students with preference list
length less than 10. We can see how this relates to the average rank a student
gives their assignment. We calculate average rank with 0 rank being a student's top choice, and
one more than the length of the preference list rank (ex. rank 6 for lists of
length 5) if a student is unmatched in the final matching.

\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.65\linewidth]{figures/rank.png}
  \caption{Comparison of average rank of assignment in each trial.}
  \label{fig:ranks}
\end{figure}

\newpage
When we compare Figures \ref{fig:unmatched} and \ref{fig:ranks}, we find that
the number of unmatched students in \textsc{greedy} significantly affects the
average rank for students across trials, which indicates that students are the
least satisfied in \textsc{greedy}. Additionally, when we look at Figure
\ref{fig:blocking_pairs} as well, we can see that there is a tradeoff in
\textsc{wt} for having so many more blocking pairs, in that students on average
receive a section they rank slightly lower than their assignments in
\textsc{fcfs}. However, it does still tend to give students their second or
third choice across all years and preference list lengths.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.65\linewidth]{figures/classes_violin.png}
  \caption{Comparison between sample years of distribution of students to
    class sections over all trials.}
  \label{fig:class_size}
\end{figure}

Finally, though it was not explicitly part of the design of the algorithms, we are
interested in how well the sections are balanced regardless of student groups.
Generally, we would hope all the sections are similarly sized. Figure
\ref{fig:class_size} illustrates the distribution of students in each sample
year. The most successfully balanced year is Year 3, in which the smallest class
in the \textsc{wt} trials is size 12 and the largest class is size 18, giving a
difference of 6.
Additionally, in both Year 2 and 3 the majority of classes were size 12 or 13.
While there is some range between class sizes in the \textsc{wt} algorithm, the
figure demonstrates that it is much smaller than the range in \textsc{fcfs} and
\textsc{greedy}, especially for longer length preference lists.

As a whole, the \textsc{wt} algorithm outperformed \textsc{fcfs} and
\textsc{greedy} on every metric we analyzed other than blocking pairs. Due to
our re-framing of the problem under intra-group stability from Definition
\ref{def:intra}, we accept this result as successful as we prioritize finding a
full, group fair matching.
